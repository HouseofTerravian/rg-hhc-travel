RG · Relationship Goals Introduction

Before You Begin

Most couples aren't failing because they stopped talking. They're failing because the talking stopped working. The words keep coming — in the kitchen, in the car, in text threads at 11pm — but somehow they keep missing. You talk past each other. You say things and feel unheard. You listen, but you're really just waiting.

This week you reset that pattern. Not with communication scripts or formulas — with actual practice. Each day builds a specific skill: listening without preparing your response, speaking with precision instead of volume, naming what you feel without making it an accusation.

"The goal of communication isn't to win or to be heard — it's to understand and be understood. Those are two very different things."

Give each exercise the time it needs. Rushing through these means you're back to the old pattern before you even recognize it. Both partners need to do each exercise fully — not just read it and move on.

How To Use This Guide

1

Pick a consistent time. Same time each day works better than squeezing it in. This is a practice, not a task.

2

No phones, no background noise. The exercises require actual presence. They won't work halfway.

3

Be patient with yourselves. Some of these skills are genuinely new. Clunky is fine. Trying is the whole point.

4

Write in the reflection spaces. Writing after a conversation helps you process what happened more honestly than talking through it does.

5

Keep the tools. The frameworks in this guide are yours to keep using. They work beyond this week.

Day One

The Communication Audit
You can't change a pattern you haven't named yet.
Today's Mission

Each partner spends 10 minutes — separately, in writing — answering three questions about how you currently communicate. Then share your answers out loud, without interrupting. After both have shared, together identify one pattern that you both recognize and agree needs to change this week. That pattern is your target. Write it down.

⏱ Allow 20–25 minutes · Write first, then share · No defending your answers
Why this matters: Most communication problems are patterns — not incidents. The same cycle repeats, the same things get said, the same person shuts down or escalates. Before you can change the pattern, both partners have to see it clearly and agree it's real. The audit makes it real.
Audit Questions — Answer Separately, Then Share
💬

"When we disagree, I tend to... and I know it doesn't help because..."

💬

"The conversation we keep having that never goes anywhere is about..."

💬

"The way I communicate that I most want to change this week is..."

Reflection — Write It Down
What pattern did you both agree on? Why does it keep happening?

Day Two
👂
Deep Listening Practice
Listening is not the pause between your sentences. It's a skill. Practice it today.
Today's Mission

Partner A speaks for 5 uninterrupted minutes about something they've been carrying — anything real. Partner B's only job is to listen — not to prepare a response, not to correct facts, not to relate it to their own experience. When Partner A finishes, Partner B reflects back: "What I heard you say was..." and "What I noticed you feeling was..." Partner A confirms or corrects. Then switch. The goal is not to solve anything. The goal is to be heard.

⏱ Allow 25–30 minutes · Set a timer for each turn · Resist the urge to respond
Why this matters: Deep listening is not natural — it's a practice that goes against the instinct to respond, fix, and relate. When your partner feels genuinely heard, without editing or rebuttal, it changes the emotional temperature of your relationship. That shift is what makes everything else this week possible.
Conversation Prompts
💬

"Something I've been carrying lately that I haven't said out loud is..."

💬

"When I try to share something hard and feel unheard, what happens inside me is..."

💬

"What it felt like to be listened to the way you just listened is..."

Reflection — Write It Down
When were you tempted to interrupt? What did you notice when you didn't?

Day Three
🗣️
Emotional Vocabulary
If you can name it more precisely, your partner has a chance of actually understanding it.
Today's Mission

Most people have a very small working emotional vocabulary: happy, sad, angry, fine. Today you expand it. Each partner describes how they've felt in the past week using at least five specific, distinct emotions — not "good" or "bad," but words like overlooked, restless, relieved, tender, depleted, proud, cautious, longing. For each word, explain briefly where it came from. Share your lists. Ask clarifying questions only — no reassuring, no minimizing, no explaining away.

⏱ Allow 20–25 minutes · Aim for precision, not performance · Questions welcome
Why this matters: Vague emotional language creates vague understanding. When you say "I'm fine" or "I'm frustrated," your partner has to guess what you mean — and they'll usually guess wrong. Precision in emotion is precision in intimacy. The more accurately you can name what you feel, the more accurately your partner can respond to it.
Conversation Prompts
💬

"An emotion I've felt this week that I don't usually name out loud is... and it came from..."

💬

"An emotion I often feel with you that I've never actually named is..."

💬

"When I say I'm 'fine,' what I usually actually mean is..."

Reflection — Write It Down
Which emotion on your partner's list surprised you most? What do you want to understand better about it?

Day Four
⚡
Trigger Mapping
Your triggers are not random. Today you figure out where they actually come from.
Today's Mission

Each partner identifies two or three things their partner says or does that reliably provokes a strong reaction — and traces it. Not just what triggers you, but why: what it means to you, what it reminds you of, what fear or wound it touches. Share your maps with each other. The listener's job is to understand — not to defend their behavior or explain why they do it. Understanding the trigger is today's goal. Changing behavior comes later.

⏱ Allow 25 minutes · Listener: receive, don't defend · Questions welcome after sharing
Why this matters: Most relationship arguments aren't really about what they're about. They're about old wounds that got pressed. When you understand your partner's triggers — where they came from, what they mean — you stop accidentally detonating them. And you start communicating with the whole person, not just the version in front of you.
Conversation Prompts
💬

"Something you do that tends to send me somewhere difficult is... and the reason it hits me that way is..."

💬

"When that happens, what I'm actually afraid of underneath it is..."

💬

"One thing you could say or do differently in that moment that would actually help me is..."

Reflection — Write It Down
What did you learn today that you didn't know before? How will it change how you show up tomorrow?

Day Five
📌
The Clean Request
Most partners don't know what you actually want. Today you practice saying it directly.
Today's Mission

A clean request has three parts: what you want, why it matters to you, and how you'll know you've received it. It isn't a complaint dressed up as a request. It isn't a veiled demand or a hope disguised as a hint. Each partner makes three clean requests — about anything real. Practice the structure: "I'd like [specific thing]. It matters to me because [genuine reason]. I'll feel it when [concrete signal]." After you share, the listener asks: "Is there anything I misunderstood?"

⏱ Allow 20–25 minutes · Write your requests first · No negotiating during sharing
Why this matters: Most communication breakdowns happen because one partner is making an indirect request — through tone, through withdrawal, through criticism — and the other partner doesn't know what to do with it. A clean, direct request gives your partner something they can actually respond to. It's also an act of respect: you're treating them like someone capable of meeting your need.
Conversation Prompts
💬

"Something I've been hinting at or hoping you'd figure out, instead of just asking for, is..."

💬

"One thing I need from you that I've been afraid to ask for directly is..."

💬

"When I make this request, what I'm really hoping for between us is..."

Reflection — Write It Down
How did it feel to ask for what you actually need? What made it hard — or easier than expected?

Day Six
🔧
Repair Moves
Knowing how to come back after a rupture is more important than not rupturing at all.
Today's Mission

Together, build your personal repair kit: a short list of things each of you can say or do to de-escalate when a conversation goes sideways. Not generic platitudes — specific moves that actually work for your specific relationship. Examples: a code word to call a pause, an acknowledgment phrase that disarms instead of inflames, a physical signal (like a hand on the shoulder) that means I'm still with you. Write your kit down. Then practice one repair move on an old argument — go back to something recent that went badly, and replay it using a repair move.

⏱ Allow 25 minutes · Build the kit first, then practice · Keep the list somewhere accessible
Why this matters: All couples have difficult conversations. The ones who stay together aren't the ones who never fight — they're the ones who know how to come back. A repair move is a skill, and like all skills, it has to be practiced before you need it, not just discovered under fire.
Conversation Prompts
💬

"When things are escalating between us, the thing that actually helps me calm down is..."

💬

"Something you could say in a heated moment that would make me feel seen instead of attacked is..."

💬

"A recent argument that could have gone better — and one repair move that might have changed it..."

Reflection — Write It Down
What's in your repair kit? Write it here so you can find it when you need it.

Day Seven
💛
Appreciation Language
Communication isn't only for hard things. Today you practice using it for the good ones.
Today's Mission

Each partner writes a short appreciation — five sentences minimum — about the other. Not general praise. Specific, observed, recent. Something you witnessed this week. Something they do that you've never formally named. Something about the way they are with you that you want them to know you notice. Read them to each other, slowly. Then answer one final question together: What is one new communication habit we're committing to keep? Write it down. Sign it.

⏱ Allow 20–25 minutes · Write first, then read aloud · Receive it without deflecting
Why this matters: Good communication isn't just the absence of bad communication. It's the active, deliberate practice of saying the things that matter — including the things that feel good, true, and worth saying out loud. Appreciation, expressed specifically and regularly, is one of the most powerful things two people can exchange.
Prompts for Your Appreciation
✍️

"Something I observed you do this week that I want you to know I saw was..."

✍️

"A way you communicate that I genuinely appreciate — and don't say often enough — is..."

✍️

"The communication habit I'm committing to keep because of this week is..."
